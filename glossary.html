BJJ Glossary

Every term your kid's coach uses — explained in plain English for parents.

Positions
Position
Guard

The position where the bottom person uses their legs to control the top person. In closed guard, the legs are locked around the opponent's waist.

Position
Closed Guard

Guard with the legs fully locked (ankles crossed) around the opponent's waist.

Position
Open Guard

Guard position where the legs are not locked — more dynamic and varied.

Position
Half Guard

One of your legs is between both of the opponent's legs. A transitional position.

Position
Mount

Sitting on top of your opponent's chest or stomach, facing their head. Very dominant.

Position
Side Control

Controlling the opponent from the side, perpendicular to their body.

Position
Back Control / Back Mount

Positioned behind the opponent, chest to their back, with hooks (feet) inside their hips. The most dominant position in BJJ.

Position
Knee on Belly

Placing one knee on the opponent's stomach while the other foot is on the mat. A control position.

Position
Turtle

A defensive position where someone is on their hands and knees.

Position
North-South

Controlling the opponent from the top, facing their feet.

Techniques
Technique
Sweep

Reversing position from the bottom — using technique to go from guard to being on top.

Technique
Takedown

A technique to bring the opponent from standing to the ground.

Technique
Guard Pass

Getting past the opponent's legs to reach side control, mount, or back control.

Technique
Submission

A choke or joint lock that forces the opponent to tap out and signals the end of the match.

Technique
Tap / Tapping Out

The signal that you give up — tapping your hand on your partner or the mat. In training, tapping is encouraged and respected.

Technique
Armbar

A submission that applies pressure to the elbow joint by hyperextending the arm.

Technique
Triangle Choke

A choke applied using the legs — trapping the opponent's head and arm.

Technique
Rear Naked Choke (RNC)

A choke applied from back control, using the forearm and bicep to compress the neck.

Technique
Americana

A joint lock applied to the shoulder while the opponent is on their back.

Technique
Kimura

A shoulder lock applied from a variety of positions — one of the most common submissions in BJJ.

Technique
Shrimping (Hip Escape)

A fundamental movement — using your hips to create space and escape from bad positions.

Technique
Bridge and Roll

Arching the hips off the mat to off-balance an opponent in mount.

Competition
Competition
Points

Points are awarded for achieving dominant positions. Takedown = 2, Sweep = 2, Guard Pass = 3, Knee on Belly = 2, Mount = 4, Back Control = 4.

Competition
Advantage

A near-score. Awarded when a competitor almost achieves a scoring position. Used as a tiebreaker.

Competition
Penalty

Assessed for stalling, running away, or unsportsmanlike conduct. Also used as a tiebreaker.

Competition
Submission Win

Winning by making the opponent tap out — the highest result in BJJ competition.

Competition
Time Limit

Matches have time limits. If nobody submits, the person with the most points wins.

Competition
Absolute Division

A division where all weight classes compete together.

Competition
IBJJF

International Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu Federation — the main governing body for BJJ competition worldwide.

Gear
Gear
Gi (Kimono)

The traditional BJJ uniform — a thick jacket (called the kimono top), pants, and a belt. Required in gi competition.

Gear
No-Gi

Training or competing without the gi, wearing shorts and a rash guard.

Gear
Rash Guard

A tight-fitting athletic shirt worn under the gi jacket or in no-gi training. Protects the skin.

Gear
Belt

Worn around the waist, indicating rank. Kids have their own belt progression separate from adults.

Gear
Stripe

A piece of tape on the belt indicating progress within a rank. Most belts have up to 4 stripes before promotion.

Gear
Gi Size

Gi sizes for kids range from M000 (smallest) to M4 (largest), and adults from A0 to A5. Use our Gi Size Finder for help.

Gear
Spats

Tight-fitting leggings worn under the gi or for no-gi training.

Training
Training
Rolling

The BJJ term for sparring — live, resistant practice with a partner. Not fighting, but active practice.

Training
Drilling

Repetitive practice of a specific technique with a compliant partner. Like playing catch — not competitive, just rehearsal.

Training
Open Mat

Unstructured training time — usually free sparring with whoever is available, often open to guests from other gyms.

Training
Live Training

Full sparring at your own effort level.

Training
Tapping Early

A phrase coaches use to encourage kids to tap before they feel pain — tapping is smart, not a sign of weakness.

Training
Positional Sparring

Drilling that starts from a specific position (like mount or guard), rather than from standing.

Training
Warm-up

The beginning of class, usually with running, movements, or games that get the body ready to train.

Training
Oss

A common martial arts greeting and acknowledgment. Borrowed from Japanese martial arts. Used in many BJJ schools.

Training
Line Up

When the class forms a line by rank (white belt to highest belt) at the beginning and end of class to bow in and bow out.
