Tournament Guide

How scoring works, what to pack, what to expect, and your job as a parent on competition day.

How Scoring Works

BJJ matches are won by submission (opponent taps out) or by points. Points are awarded for achieving dominant positions. If the score is tied at the end of regulation, advantages and penalties are used as tiebreakers.

What Happens Points Why It Matters
Takedown 2 pts Taking opponent from standing to the ground
Sweep 2 pts Reversing from bottom to top
Knee on Belly 2 pts Controlling with knee on stomach
Guard Pass 3 pts Getting past opponent's legs to a dominant position
Mount 4 pts Sitting on opponent's chest — very dominant
Back Control with Hooks 4 pts Controlling from behind with feet inside hips — most dominant position
Submission Win instantly Choke or joint lock — opponent taps. Match is over.

What to Expect at a Tournament

  • Weigh-ins happen before competing. Whether the gi is on or off during weigh-ins depends on the organization — check the ruleset in advance.
  • Matches are short: kids matches are often 2–4 minutes depending on age and belt level.
  • The bracket is usually single elimination or round robin. Your child may compete multiple times, or just once.
  • Your kid may win their first match, lose, or never get their first match if someone doesn't show. All of this is normal — tournaments are unpredictable.
  • Coaches or parents may be allowed to stand near the mat. Know the specific rules before you get there — some events restrict this.
  • Your job on the sideline: cheer positively, stay calm, and don't coach from the stands unless your gym coach says it's OK. The coach on the mat has it handled.

Mat Etiquette — Know Before You Go

  • Bow when stepping on and off the mat
  • No shoes on the mat, ever
  • Trim your child's nails before competition
  • Remove all jewelry before training and competition
  • Wash the gi before every class and tournament — a clean gi is required and shows respect for opponents

Tournament Day Packing List

The Gi Bag

  • Clean, ironed gi (presentation matters for first impressions)
  • Backup gi if possible
  • Rash guard (worn under the gi jacket)
  • Spare belt
  • Flip flops (no bare feet off the mat)
  • Towel and small bag for dirty gear after competing

Food and Hydration

  • Water bottle — fill it up and refill throughout the day
  • Light snacks before competing (banana, granola bar, crackers)
  • Real food after competing — your kid will be hungry
  • Avoid heavy meals right before a match

For You (the Parent)

  • Phone charger — tournaments run long and your battery will die
  • Comfortable shoes — you'll be standing for hours
  • Cash — many tournaments have food vendors and merchandise tables
  • Patience — tournaments run late, brackets change, things happen. That's part of it.

Tiebreakers: Advantages and Penalties

  • Advantage: A near-score awarded when a competitor nearly achieves a scoring position. If the score is tied at the end, the athlete with more advantages wins.
  • Penalty: Given for stalling, fleeing, or unsportsmanlike conduct. If everything else is tied, fewer penalties wins.
  • If it's still tied after advantages and penalties, the referee may declare a decision or the match may go to overtime depending on the ruleset.
