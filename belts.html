BJJ Belt Guide

Understanding the kids' belt system, how stripes work, and what comes next.

Note: The kids' belt system in BJJ is separate from the adult system. Children (typically under 16) progress through a dedicated color belt sequence before transitioning to the adult progression at Blue Belt. This page covers both systems.

How Stripes Work

Each belt can have up to 4 stripes before promotion. Stripes are small pieces of tape applied to the tip of the belt by the instructor. They mark progress and effort within each rank — your child can earn 1, 2, 3, or 4 stripes before receiving the next belt.

Not every school uses all 4 stripes, and timing varies by gym and instructor. What matters is that each stripe represents real progress on the mat.

Kids Belt System (IBJJF)

Under the IBJJF system, children work through a dedicated color belt progression. The two-tone belts (like grey/white) are transitional — they sit between solid colors and give kids shorter-term milestone goals to work toward.

White Belt
The starting point for all kids — learning basic positions and movements.
Grey/White Belt
First transition — grey belt with a white center bar.
Grey Belt
Solid grey belt, showing consistent fundamentals.
Grey/Black Belt
Grey belt with a black center bar.
Yellow/White Belt
Moving into the yellow sequence — yellow with white bar.
Yellow Belt
Solid yellow — growing technique and positional awareness.
Yellow/Black Belt
Yellow with a black center bar.
Orange/White Belt
Orange sequence begins — orange with a white bar.
Orange Belt
Solid orange — typically more fluid on the mat with several techniques.
Orange/Black Belt
Orange with a black center bar.
Green/White Belt
The final kids color begins — green with a white bar.
Green Belt
Solid green — advanced kid level, typically competing well.
Green/Black Belt
Highest kids belt — on the cusp of transitioning to adult belts at age 16.

Adult Belt System (16 and older)

At age 16, students can begin earning adult belts. Most kids restart at White Belt when transitioning, though some schools recognize prior experience. The adult belt progression is one of the longest in martial arts — it can take a decade or more to earn a Black Belt.

White Belt
Starting point for adult beginners, and where most kids restart at 16.
Blue Belt
Typically earned after 1–2 years. A real milestone — demonstrates solid foundational knowledge.
Purple Belt
Advanced — often 3–5 years in. Beginning to develop a personal game.
Brown Belt
Expert level. Refining and mastering technique over years of practice.
Black Belt
A lifelong achievement. Most practitioners take 10+ years to earn this.
